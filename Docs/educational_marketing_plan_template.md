# Educational Marketing Plan Template For School Safety Tools

This template helps students explain an educational safety tool responsibly. It is for supervised classroom planning, public communication practice, and project review. It is not a sales deck, a funding request, or proof that a tool is ready for real-world enforcement.

## 1. Problem Statement

Describe the classroom problem in one paragraph.

- What safety or awareness topic is the project teaching?
- Who is affected by the problem in a school or training context?
- What does the learner need to understand after using the tool?
- What evidence shows that this topic is useful for education?

## 2. Intended Audience

Name the audience before making any claim.

- Students: age range, course level, and expected supervision.
- Teachers: classroom use, discussion guide, and evaluation needs.
- Families or community mentors: optional awareness support.
- Administrators: deployment, safety boundary, and privacy expectations.

## 3. Ethical Boundary

State the boundary plainly.

- The tool is for supervised cybersecurity education and fraud-awareness training.
- The tool must not accuse, identify, monitor, or punish people.
- The tool must not be used for live enforcement or unsupervised decision-making.
- A teacher, mentor, or qualified reviewer remains responsible for interpretation.

## 4. Safety And Privacy Claims

Only make claims that can be demonstrated.

- Explain what data is used in classroom exercises.
- Prefer synthetic, sample, or consent-based training data.
- State what data is not collected.
- State whether any browser, account, or external AI workflow is optional or required.
- Avoid absolute promises such as perfect detection, guaranteed protection, or zero risk.

## 5. Evidence Needed Before Public Claims

Before publishing a public claim, collect the evidence that supports it.

- Test results: what was tested, by whom, and with which version.
- Classroom review: teacher feedback, student comprehension notes, and known limits.
- Privacy review: data handled, retention rule, and consent requirement.
- Safety review: misuse risks and mitigation steps.
- Accessibility review: readability, keyboard use, contrast, and language clarity.

## 6. Launch Checklist

Use this checklist before presenting the project outside the classroom.

- The README describes the educational purpose.
- The project states pre-alpha, alpha, beta, or stable status.
- The project includes a clear misuse boundary.
- The project avoids real accusations or real personal data.
- A teacher or mentor has reviewed the public description.
- Known limitations are visible.
- Feedback channels are listed.

## 7. Feedback Loop

Plan how the project will improve without overclaiming.

- Collect learner feedback after exercises.
- Track confusing screens, unclear wording, and safety concerns.
- Record which examples worked and which examples should be removed.
- Update the project notes before asking new students to use it.
- Keep a changelog of public wording changes.

## 8. What Not To Do

Do not include private personal planning, creator payment arrangements, political influence plans, payment processor details, or private life-planning material in a public educational repository.

Do not claim that the tool proves guilt, detects every unsafe situation, replaces a professional reviewer, or protects a community without verified evidence.

Do not present visibility, attention, badges, or early support as scientific validation or operational readiness.
